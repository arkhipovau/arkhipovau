Space, Time, and Light "My works always begin with an undefined sense and intuition, something that doesn't yet exist in the form of words. Then, I try to articulate this feeling, transform it into a concrete idea, which I then describe in words, sketch, or create a model before attempting to apply this idea in real space. I attach great importance to this initial indefinite reflection, striving to experience it for as long as possible before moving on to a more concrete form. And inspiration comes from the physical experience of my body in this space, which I learned to feel well during my teenage years of breakdancing. For example, with the waterfalls in New York: I often felt, when I visited New York, that it was very difficult for me to sense the size of the skyscrapers—they were so disproportionate to me. But when I added waterfalls, it was as if I restored a more familiar scale to the city." — Olafur Eliasson

Color Perception and Physical Experience Often, viewers' color perception changes, making the physical experience border on the psycho-physical. The viewer becomes an active participant in the experiment, interpreting art as their own experience. Meanings and their interpretation remain within the realm of the viewer's responsibility. Olafur creates "Garden of Situations," where corporeality, tangibility, and visual perception are questioned. In a space that seems apocalyptic, one still feels inclined to linger. The weather makes nature tactile and creates a collective experience: here, unlike the individualistic approach to personal perception, viewers are invited to share the experience and feel part of a society.

Olafur Eliasson. The Weather Project. 2003. Installation at Tate Modern, London, United Kingdom.

Mirrors in his works do not appear with the aim of confusing or bewildering the viewer. Unlike magicians, Olafur aims to expose techniques and express primarily the nature of humans and their perception models.

Land Art and Its Inversion Land art is a form of artistic expression where works are created in the natural environment, using materials found in the surrounding nature and interacting with the landscape to create visually stunning compositions. Catalogs of photographs of natural landscapes in Iceland, his native country, become sources of ideas for his works. The author extracts elements of nature, mixes them, and places them in an optical play.

Olafur Eliasson. Flower pavilion, 2003. 5th Shenzhen International Public Art Exhibition, China.

Olafur Eliasson. The very large ice step experienced. 1998. Nanterre, Paris.

Olafur Eliasson. Orientation lights for rising seas. 2023.

Icelandic ice is often the subject of Olafur's research, besides the natural and ecological contexts, raising the question of temporality. Olafur Eliasson's relationship with land art is interesting. The artist works more on the opposite plane of this concept: he introduces natural objects or elements into indoor spaces.

Olafur Eliasson. The very large ice floor. 1998. XXIV Bienal de São Paulo.

Olafur Elliasson. Reversed waterfall, 1998. Leeum, Samsung Museum of Art, Seoul. 2016.

The romantic idea of nature as the highest reality and ultimate truth is not close to Olafur; for him, it is one facet and point of view, an act of seeing, a source of beauty. The key is precisely the act of seeing, and the object can be both natural and artificial.
